# Journal Positioning

## Current Primary Article

Clinical Cognition Transformation: Studying Human-AI Clinical Cognition in Distributed Healthcare Systems

## Contribution

The repository should now be positioned as a research program on Human-AI Clinical Cognition, not as a dental AI infrastructure or DCS standards proposal.

CCTL's central argument is that clinical cognition is no longer exclusively an individual cognitive activity. It is increasingly a distributed process emerging from interactions among clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, institutions, and patients.

The scientific challenge is no longer understanding clinical reasoning alone. The scientific challenge is understanding how clinical cognition transforms within distributed human-AI systems.

## Research Framing

The paper should define Clinical Cognition Transformation as a field-level research agenda for studying distributed human-AI clinical cognitive systems.

Core concepts:

- Human-AI Clinical Cognition;
- distributed human-AI clinical cognitive systems;
- cognitive provenance;
- cognitive reconstruction;
- longitudinal cognitive change;
- human-AI co-cognition;
- clinical reasoning transformation as a subproblem of clinical cognition transformation.

## Candidate Research Questions

- How does clinical cognition transform as it moves through clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, workflows, institutions, patients, and caregivers?
- How much cognition can be reconstructed from downstream artifacts after the original reasoning event disappears?
- Which forms of clinical cognition are preserved, compressed, delegated, translated, simplified, reframed, reconstructed, redistributed, reassigned, or transformed?
- How do clinicians' cognitive abilities change longitudinally as AI becomes embedded into daily practice?
- How can cognitive provenance be represented and audited without reducing clinical cognition to a software log?

## Role of DCS in Publication

DCS should be presented as an origin case and a candidate cognitive provenance representation layer.

It should not be positioned as the primary contribution, the central research question, or the final artifact. The dental packet and DCS work explain how the broader research question emerged: attempts to make clinical context AI-ready revealed that the deeper issue was transformation of clinical cognition across distributed human-AI systems.

## Suggested Paper Structure

1. Background: clinical reasoning, distributed cognition, and AI-embedded healthcare.
2. Problem: clinical cognition is moving across humans, AI systems, documents, workflows, institutions, patients, and caregivers.
3. Definition: Human-AI Clinical Cognition.
4. Thesis: Clinical Cognition Transformation as the central research problem.
5. Taxonomy: preservation, compression, delegation, translation, simplification, reframing, reconstruction, redistribution, reassignment, transformation.
6. Research programs: Clinical Cognition Transformation, Cognitive Reconstruction, Human-AI Co-Cognition, Cognitive Provenance, Longitudinal Cognitive Change.
7. Origins: AI-ready Dental Case Packet, DCS, Reasoning Decay, Clinical Reasoning Reconstruction, Human-AI Co-Reasoning, Clinical Reasoning Transformation.
8. Methods: synthetic workflow simulation, published case reconstruction, real workflow artifact studies, longitudinal human-AI cognitive studies.
9. Implications: future clinical expertise, accountability, documentation, patient participation, and institutional design.
