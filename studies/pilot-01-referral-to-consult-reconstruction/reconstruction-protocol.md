# Reconstruction Protocol

This protocol provides a step-by-step method for reconstructing clinical cognition from a referral-consult artifact pair. The output is a reconstruction hypothesis, not a claim of full access to the original reasoning event.

## Step 1: Read Referral Artifact

Read the referral letter without looking at the consultation note. Extract the referral reason, suspected diagnosis, uncertainty statements, requested consultation, contextual details, and timeline.

Example: A referral says, "persistent jaw pain after extraction, concern for infection versus delayed healing, please evaluate." The source artifact includes symptom persistence, a suspected infectious mechanism, and explicit uncertainty.

## Step 2: Infer Likely Source Cognition

Infer the cognition that the referral artifact most likely represents. Identify the apparent clinical concern, working frame, uncertainty, and requested cognitive labor.

Example: The referring clinician may be asking the specialist to distinguish normal healing from infection and to determine whether intervention is needed.

## Step 3: Read Consultation Artifact

Read the specialist consultation note. Extract the final assessment, differential diagnosis, treatment recommendation, uncertainty statements, rationale, and follow-up plan.

Example: The specialist concludes that symptoms are more consistent with neuropathic pain than infection and recommends conservative management plus follow-up.

## Step 4: Identify Preserved Cognition

Mark elements that remain traceable from referral to consultation.

Example: Persistent pain remains the central clinical concern in both artifacts.

## Step 5: Identify Transformed Cognition

Mark elements that change form, emphasis, mechanism, or clinical meaning.

Example: The concern shifts from infection versus delayed healing to neuropathic pain after dental procedure.

## Step 6: Identify Missing Cognition

Mark reasoning that appears necessary but is absent from one or both artifacts.

Example: The referral requests evaluation but does not explain why infection is suspected, such as fever, swelling, drainage, or imaging findings.

## Step 7: Identify Newly Introduced Cognition

Mark claims, mechanisms, diagnoses, or recommendations that appear in the consultation note without a clear source in the referral.

Example: The specialist introduces medication-related osteonecrosis risk based on history not present in the referral.

## Step 8: Characterize Drift

Describe how cognition changed across the transition.

Example drift labels: diagnostic reframing, uncertainty compression, mechanism substitution, treatment intent shift, responsibility reassignment.

## Step 9: Generate Reconstruction Hypothesis

Write a short reconstruction hypothesis that states what source cognition likely existed, what survived, what changed, what is missing, and how confident the reconstruction is.

Example: "The referral likely encoded concern for a post-procedural complication with uncertainty between infection and healing delay. The consultation preserved the symptom concern but reframed the mechanism toward neuropathic pain. The original evidentiary basis for infection concern is not recoverable from the referral alone. Confidence: moderate for concern preservation, low for original causal reasoning."
