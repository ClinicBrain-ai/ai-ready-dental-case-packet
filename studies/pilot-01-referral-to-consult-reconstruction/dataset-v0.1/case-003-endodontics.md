## Referral Letter

Persistent percussion sensitivity on previously root-treated maxillary premolar.

### Suspected Diagnosis

Persistent apical periodontitis versus vertical root fracture.

### Uncertainty Statements

Radiographic changes are subtle. I cannot determine whether this is endodontic failure, fracture, or occlusal trauma.

### Requested Consultation

Please assess restorability and advise retreatment versus extraction.

### Contextual Details

Root canal treatment was completed four years ago. Crown placed three years ago. Patient reports pain on biting. Periapical radiograph shows slight widening of the periodontal ligament space.

### Timeline

Four years post-root canal, two months of biting discomfort, referred after occlusal adjustment did not resolve symptoms.

## Specialist Consultation Note

### Final Assessment

Suspected vertical root fracture with guarded prognosis.

### Differential Diagnosis

Vertical root fracture, persistent apical periodontitis, missed canal, occlusal trauma.

### Treatment Recommendation

Limited-field CBCT and periodontal probing. If fracture is confirmed, extraction is recommended. If not confirmed, nonsurgical retreatment may be considered.

### Uncertainty Statements

Fracture is suspected but not definitively confirmed on two-dimensional imaging.

### Rationale

Localized deep probing and biting pain pattern increase suspicion for fracture. Imaging is needed to evaluate root integrity.

### Follow-up Plan

Obtain CBCT, discuss prognosis, and decide between retreatment and extraction based on fracture evidence.

## Ground Truth Reasoning Notes

The referring clinician recognized a prognosis fork: retreatment may help endodontic failure but would not solve vertical fracture. Referral was driven by inability to distinguish subtle apical pathology from fracture using routine exam and radiography.
