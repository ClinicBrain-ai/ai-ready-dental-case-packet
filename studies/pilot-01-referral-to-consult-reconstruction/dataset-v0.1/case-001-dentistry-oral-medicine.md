# Case 001: Persistent Oral Ulcer

Synthetic educational case. No real patient information.

## Case ID

P1-V01-001

## Specialty Domain

Dentistry -> Oral medicine.

## Referral Letter

### Referral Reason

Persistent ulcer on the left lateral tongue for three weeks.

### Suspected Diagnosis

Traumatic ulcer from sharp molar cusp versus aphthous ulcer. Oral dysplasia cannot be excluded.

### Uncertainty Statements

The lesion has not resolved as expected. I am uncertain whether the persistent appearance is due to ongoing trauma or a premalignant process.

### Requested Consultation

Please evaluate the ulcer and advise whether biopsy is indicated.

### Contextual Details

Adult patient reports intermittent irritation from a rough lower molar. No fever or generalized oral lesions. The ulcer has a firm edge on palpation and has not improved after smoothing the cusp and recommending topical care.

### Timeline

Week 0: patient noticed tongue soreness. Week 1: general dentist identified rough cusp and smoothed it. Week 3: ulcer persists, referral sent.

## Specialist Consultation Note

### Final Assessment

Non-healing lateral tongue ulcer with indurated border. Biopsy recommended to rule out epithelial dysplasia or early squamous cell carcinoma.

### Differential Diagnosis

Traumatic ulcer, epithelial dysplasia, early squamous cell carcinoma, chronic aphthous ulcer.

### Treatment Recommendation

Incisional biopsy within one week. Continue avoidance of local trauma and return for pathology review.

### Uncertainty Statements

Trauma remains possible, but persistence after removal of the irritant and induration increase concern for dysplasia or malignancy.

### Rationale

The duration, location, and firm border make simple trauma less secure as the sole explanation. Histologic evaluation is needed.

### Follow-up Plan

Biopsy appointment scheduled. Review pathology and coordinate definitive management if dysplasia or carcinoma is identified.

## Ground Truth Reasoning Notes

The referring clinician suspected a common traumatic ulcer but recognized that persistence after smoothing the tooth changed the risk profile. The referral was motivated by uncertainty between benign trauma and early neoplastic change. The rationale for referral was not certainty of cancer, but concern that the ulcer had failed to follow the expected healing pathway.
