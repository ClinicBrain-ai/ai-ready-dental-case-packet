# Future Paper

## Working Title

Can Clinical Cognition Be Reconstructed from Referral Letters and Specialist Consultation Notes?

## Abstract Sketch

Clinical cognition often disappears as a live event while leaving traces in clinical artifacts. Referral letters and specialist consultation notes provide a natural artifact pair for studying how cognition is preserved, transformed, compressed, or newly introduced across clinical handoffs. This paper would report a pilot method for reconstructing clinical cognition from referral-consult pairs using a structured artifact schema, coding framework, and evaluation rubric. The goal is not to recover the original reasoning event completely, but to test whether disciplined partial reconstruction is feasible.

## Research Question

How much clinical cognition can be reconstructed from the transition between referral letters and specialist consultation notes?

## Methods Overview

The study would assemble a small dataset of public, educational, or simulated referral-consult pairs. Reviewers would extract artifact fields, infer likely source cognition from the referral letter, compare it with the specialist consultation note, and code preservation, compression, delegation, translation, reframing, diagnostic replacement, mechanism substitution, uncertainty loss, new cognition, and missing cognition.

Pairs would be scored with a 0-4 rubric across reasoning preservation, uncertainty preservation, treatment intent preservation, cognitive drift, and introduced reasoning. Disagreements between reviewers would be analyzed as indicators of artifact ambiguity or coding-rule weakness.

## Expected Findings

- Some clinical cognition is likely reconstructable from artifact pairs.
- Diagnostic concerns may be more preserved than uncertainty or causal reasoning.
- Consultation notes may introduce new cognition that is not traceable to referral artifacts.
- Missing cognition may be common when referral letters are sparse.
- Reconstruction confidence will vary substantially by artifact quality.

## Limitations

- The method cannot recover private clinician thought.
- Public and educational cases may not represent real referral workflows.
- Simulated cases may overstate reconstruction feasibility.
- Small pilot datasets cannot support broad claims about specialties or outcomes.
- Artifact analysis should be paired with real workflow studies in later phases.

## Intended Contribution

The paper would provide a minimally executable method for studying Cognitive Reconstruction within CCTL. It would show how clinical cognition can be studied after the original reasoning event has disappeared, while keeping uncertainty and methodological limits visible.
