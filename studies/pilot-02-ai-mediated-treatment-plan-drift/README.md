# AI-Mediated Treatment Plan Drift

Positioning: initial pilot study for exploring clinical cognition transformation.

## Background

AI-generated summaries, recommendations, and planning aids may influence downstream treatment decisions. The effect may be useful, harmful, neutral, or mixed. This pilot studies how treatment plans change after AI mediation without assuming that change is automatically error.

The study connects CCTL's interest in Human-AI Clinical Cognition with testable questions about recommendation drift, uncertainty compression, diagnostic reframing, and mechanism substitution.

## Research Question

How do AI-generated summaries, recommendations, or planning aids alter downstream treatment decisions?

## Study Design

Use a controlled case-review design with existing or synthetic clinical cases.

Participants first create or evaluate a treatment plan from source clinical material. They then review an AI-generated summary, recommendation, or planning aid and produce a revised plan. Researchers compare pre-AI and post-AI plans.

The study can begin with simulated cases to avoid patient-risk and data-access barriers.

## Types of Drift

- Recommendation drift: the treatment recommendation changes after AI mediation.
- Uncertainty compression: tentative reasoning becomes more definitive.
- Diagnostic reframing: the organizing diagnosis or clinical problem changes.
- Mechanism substitution: the causal explanation for the plan changes.
- Priority drift: urgency, sequencing, or ranking of treatment options changes.
- Responsibility shift: the plan relies more heavily on AI output or downstream review.

## Measurement Strategy

Compare pre-AI and post-AI plans for:

- treatment selected;
- rationale provided;
- diagnostic frame;
- uncertainty language;
- evidence cited;
- contraindications or alternatives considered;
- clinician confidence;
- explicit acceptance, rejection, or modification of AI recommendations.

Optional measures include reviewer-rated plan quality, calibration, and safety concerns.

## Expected Findings

The pilot may find that AI mediation changes plans even when source facts remain constant. Some changes may improve completeness or consistency. Others may compress uncertainty, over-prioritize AI-surfaced evidence, or introduce unsupported rationales.

The expected contribution is a drift typology and a feasibility assessment, not a definitive judgment about AI benefit or harm.

## Risks and Limitations

- Synthetic cases may not capture real workflow pressure.
- Participants may behave differently in study conditions than in practice.
- AI output quality may vary by model, prompt, and domain.
- Drift may be difficult to classify when multiple plan changes occur together.
- The study may identify cognitive influence without proving clinical outcome effects.

## Ethical Considerations

- Use synthetic or de-identified cases for initial pilots.
- Do not allow study-generated AI recommendations to guide live patient care.
- Disclose AI involvement to participants.
- Avoid framing participant changes as errors without clinical adjudication.
- Preserve uncertainty and disagreement in analysis.

## Minimal Viable Study Design

Create 5 to 10 short clinical cases with enough detail to support treatment planning. Ask 5 to 10 clinicians or advanced trainees to produce an initial plan, then review an AI-generated summary or recommendation and revise the plan. Annotate changes using the drift categories above.

The minimal output is a map of how AI mediation changes treatment planning artifacts under controlled conditions.
