# Longitudinal Clinician Cognition Study

Positioning: initial pilot study for exploring clinical cognition transformation.

## Background

Repeated AI use may change clinician cognition over time. The change may include cognitive delegation, expertise compression, metacognitive amplification, diagnostic workflow changes, or genuine skill atrophy. This pilot defines a realistic starting point for observing those changes longitudinally.

The study aligns with CCTL's view that the question is not whether AI necessarily damages expertise. The question is how expertise changes when clinical cognition becomes distributed across humans, AI systems, documentation artifacts, and workflows.

## Research Question

How does clinician cognition evolve with repeated AI use over time?

## Candidate Cognitive Changes

- Cognitive delegation: clinicians assign more summarization, search, ranking, or first-pass interpretation to AI.
- Expertise compression: intermediate reasoning becomes shorter or less visible.
- Metacognitive amplification: clinicians become better at supervising, challenging, or calibrating AI output.
- Diagnostic workflow changes: clinicians alter when they generate hypotheses, consult AI, or revise plans.
- Uncertainty behavior changes: clinicians preserve, compress, or outsource uncertainty differently.
- Documentation behavior changes: clinicians shift from writing reasoning to editing AI-generated reasoning.

## Possible Measurements

- Unaided reasoning tasks at baseline and follow-up.
- AI-assisted reasoning tasks at baseline and follow-up.
- Oversight tasks requiring detection of flawed AI output.
- Documentation samples before and after repeated AI exposure.
- Self-report on trust, confidence, workload, and perceived delegation.
- Time-to-decision and number of alternatives considered.
- Uncertainty language and rationale completeness.

## Study Design Options

Option 1: short longitudinal simulation. Participants complete weekly case tasks with and without AI support for 4 to 8 weeks.

Option 2: observational workflow study. Clinicians who already use AI tools contribute de-identified documentation samples and structured reflections over time.

Option 3: trainee-focused study. Trainees complete repeated clinical reasoning exercises with controlled AI exposure, allowing comparison between unaided reasoning, assisted reasoning, and oversight skill.

## Risks and Limitations

- True expertise change may require longer observation than a pilot permits.
- Practice effects may be mistaken for AI effects.
- Clinicians may vary widely in baseline AI familiarity.
- Self-report may not match actual cognitive behavior.
- Controlled tasks may not represent real clinical pressure.

## Ethical Considerations

- Do not use AI study outputs for live patient care in early pilots.
- Protect participant privacy and professional identity.
- Avoid using results for punitive evaluation of clinicians or trainees.
- Make AI exposure and study purpose explicit.
- Treat skill atrophy, transformation, and adaptation as empirical possibilities.

## Minimal Viable Study Design

Recruit 5 to 10 clinicians or advanced trainees for a 4-week simulation. Each week, participants complete one unaided case, one AI-assisted case, and one oversight task involving flawed or incomplete AI output. Collect short documentation artifacts and confidence ratings.

The minimal output is a preliminary profile of how repeated AI exposure affects reasoning visibility, delegation, uncertainty handling, and oversight performance over time.
