# Project Evolution

This repository did not begin as CCTL. It evolved through several research framings, each of which revealed a broader problem.

AI-ready Dental Case Packet  
-> Dental Context Specification (DCS)  
-> Reasoning Decay  
-> Clinical Reasoning Reconstruction  
-> Human-AI Co-Reasoning  
-> Clinical Reasoning Transformation  
-> Clinical Cognition Transformation

## AI-ready Dental Case Packet

What the project thought the problem was:

Dental AI systems needed better structured case material. The initial problem appeared to be how to package clinical records, imaging context, treatment history, annotations, and supporting evidence into an AI-ready case packet.

What was learned:

The packaging problem exposed a deeper issue. Clinical meaning was not contained in data alone. The reasoning that connected data to claims, uncertainty, and action was often implicit, fragmented, or missing.

Why the next framing became necessary:

A case packet could move information, but it did not by itself preserve clinical reasoning. This led to the Dental Context Specification.

## Dental Context Specification

What the project thought the problem was:

The project reframed around a minimal claim provenance layer for dental agent handoffs. DCS was designed to preserve claims, evidence, uncertainty, contradictions, provenance, and review state across systems.

What was learned:

DCS clarified that the key issue was not merely data interoperability. The more important problem was whether clinical reasoning remained traceable as it moved across agents, documents, and workflows.

Why the next framing became necessary:

If reasoning could be lost or distorted across handoffs, the phenomenon itself needed study. This led to Reasoning Decay.

## Reasoning Decay

What the project thought the problem was:

The project began studying whether downstream artifacts preserve conclusions while losing the reasoning that produced them.

What was learned:

Not all change is decay. Some changes are compression, translation, simplification, delegation, reframing, or necessary adaptation to context.

Why the next framing became necessary:

The project needed methods for reconstructing what cognition survived and what changed. This led to Clinical Reasoning Reconstruction.

## Clinical Reasoning Reconstruction

What the project thought the problem was:

The project asked whether original clinical reasoning could be reconstructed from published cases, referral letters, consultation notes, treatment plans, and other downstream artifacts.

What was learned:

Reconstruction was not just a measurement method. It revealed that clinical cognition leaves partial traces across documents and workflows. Some traces are recoverable; others are erased or transformed.

Why the next framing became necessary:

As AI entered the workflow, reconstruction had to account for cognition jointly produced by humans and machines. This led to Human-AI Co-Reasoning.

## Human-AI Co-Reasoning

What the project thought the problem was:

The project asked how humans and AI systems jointly produce reasoning in clinical workflows.

What was learned:

The term reasoning was useful but too narrow. Clinical work involves attention, framing, memory, documentation, explanation, coordination, responsibility, institutional constraint, patient interpretation, and longitudinal expertise.

Why the next framing became necessary:

The broader object was not only co-reasoning. It was transformation in the cognitive system itself. This led to Clinical Reasoning Transformation.

## Clinical Reasoning Transformation

What the project thought the problem was:

The project studied how reasoning is preserved, transformed, compressed, delegated, reconstructed, translated, simplified, or reassigned across human-AI workflows.

What was learned:

Clinical reasoning is one part of a larger phenomenon: clinical cognition distributed across people, AI systems, documents, institutions, and patients.

Why the next framing became necessary:

The project needed a parent identity broad enough to study the full cognitive system. This led to Clinical Cognition Transformation.

## Clinical Cognition Transformation

What the project now understands the problem to be:

The central problem is Human-AI Clinical Cognition: the distributed cognitive processes that emerge when clinical decisions are jointly produced by clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, institutional workflows, healthcare organizations, patients, and caregivers.

What was learned:

The dental packet, DCS, reasoning decay, reconstruction, and co-reasoning work were not separate projects. They were stages in discovering that clinical cognition itself is changing.

The project ultimately concluded that the central problem is not dental data exchange, AI tooling, interoperability, or ontology design. The central problem is understanding how clinical cognition changes when intelligence becomes distributed across humans, AI systems, documents, institutions, and patients.
