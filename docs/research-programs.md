# Research Programs

CCTL organizes its empirical and methodological work across four layers.

## Layer 1 - Synthetic Human-AI Workflow Simulation

This layer explores future cognitive ecosystems through simulated workflows.

Synthetic studies allow CCTL to prototype possible human-AI clinical systems before they exist in stable operational form. They can reveal candidate transformation patterns, stress-test annotation schemes, and identify questions for later empirical work.

This layer does not validate real-world effects by itself. It generates hypotheses about distributed Human-AI Clinical Cognition.

## Layer 2 - Published Case Reconstruction

This layer tests cognition reconstruction using published clinical literature.

Published cases provide publicly available artifacts in which clinical reasoning is partially preserved, edited, compressed, or retrospectively narrated. They allow early testing of reconstruction methods without requiring access to private clinical records.

This layer asks what can and cannot be recovered from the final published account of a case.

## Layer 3 - Real Workflow Artifact Studies

This layer studies cognition preservation and transformation in real healthcare workflows.

High-value artifact pairs include:

- Referral Letter <-> Specialist Consultation Note;
- Primary Assessment <-> Final Treatment Plan;
- Clinical Documentation <-> AI Interpretation.

These studies compare upstream and downstream artifacts to identify what cognition survives, what changes, what is compressed, what is delegated, and what becomes unrecoverable.

## Layer 4 - Longitudinal Human-AI Cognitive Studies

This layer observes cognitive evolution as AI becomes embedded into clinical practice.

The focus is how clinicians, teams, workflows, and patients change over time. Longitudinal studies can examine which abilities are amplified, which are delegated, which become obsolete, and which new forms of expertise emerge.

This layer treats expertise as a moving target in AI-embedded healthcare.
