# Clinical Cognition Transformation Lab (CCTL)

## Manifesto v1.0

## Studying Human-AI Clinical Cognition

Clinical cognition is changing because healthcare cognition is changing its location.

For much of modern clinical science, the central unit of analysis has been the individual clinician reasoning through a patient problem. That unit remains important, but it is no longer sufficient. Clinical decisions are increasingly produced by distributed systems: clinicians interpret, AI systems summarize or recommend, documentation artifacts preserve or distort prior thinking, institutional workflows channel attention, organizations define incentives and constraints, and patients and caregivers participate in interpretation and action.

CCTL studies this distributed cognitive system.

## Why Individual-Clinician Reasoning Is No Longer Enough

Individual clinicians still reason, judge, notice, explain, and decide. Yet many clinically important cognitive events now occur outside any single person's mind.

A diagnostic label may be introduced by an AI-generated summary. A referral letter may compress uncertainty into a single concern. A consultation note may reconstruct a reasoning pathway that was never explicitly documented. A treatment plan may convert probabilistic reasoning into operational sequencing. A patient-facing explanation may simplify a differential diagnosis into a usable decision. An institutional workflow may reassign who notices risk, who acts, and who remains accountable.

Studying only the clinician misses the transformations that occur when cognition moves across artifacts, systems, and organizational boundaries.

## AI as Part of Cognition

AI is often described as a tool. That description is useful but incomplete.

When an AI system retrieves prior data, generates a differential diagnosis, summarizes a consultation, flags a risk, drafts patient language, ranks options, or changes what a clinician attends to, it participates in the cognitive system. It may not have responsibility, professional judgment, or clinical authority in the human sense. But it can still reshape the production, preservation, delegation, and interpretation of clinical cognition.

CCTL therefore studies AI as a participant in distributed cognition, not only as a predictive model whose performance can be measured in isolation.

## Cognition Moves Through Systems

Clinical cognition does not remain stable as it moves.

It may be:

- preserved when a rationale remains traceable;
- compressed when uncertainty is reduced to a recommendation;
- delegated when an AI system performs pattern recognition or documentation synthesis;
- translated when specialist reasoning becomes patient-facing explanation;
- simplified when workflow needs require operational action;
- reframed when a downstream actor changes the organizing diagnosis;
- reconstructed when later reviewers infer prior reasoning from incomplete artifacts;
- redistributed when tasks move between clinicians, AI systems, patients, and institutions;
- reassigned when accountability shifts across roles;
- transformed when the cognitive structure of the case changes.

These transformations can be beneficial, harmful, necessary, invisible, or ambiguous. They require study rather than assumption.

## Why CCTL Studies Transformation

AI performance is important, but performance metrics alone do not explain what happens to clinical cognition.

A system can be accurate while compressing uncertainty. A summary can be fluent while erasing the reasoning path. A recommendation can be helpful while changing clinician expertise over time. A workflow can improve throughput while redistributing cognitive labor in ways that alter responsibility and training. A patient-facing explanation can improve understanding while changing the problem frame.

CCTL studies transformation because the scientific problem is not only whether AI works. The problem is how clinical cognition changes when intelligence becomes distributed across humans, AI systems, documents, workflows, organizations, patients, and caregivers.

## Research Commitments

CCTL is committed to studying Human-AI Clinical Cognition as a distributed phenomenon.

The lab focuses on:

- Clinical Cognition Transformation;
- Cognitive Reconstruction;
- Human-AI Co-Cognition;
- Cognitive Provenance;
- Longitudinal Cognitive Change;
- Future Clinical Expertise.

The aim is not to defend any single software standard, ontology, or infrastructure project. The aim is to understand how clinical cognition transforms, how those transformations can be observed, and how healthcare can preserve accountability, expertise, and meaning as cognition becomes distributed.

## Origins

CCTL emerged from work on the AI-ready Dental Case Packet, the Dental Context Specification, Reasoning Decay, Clinical Reasoning Reconstruction, Human-AI Co-Reasoning, and Clinical Reasoning Transformation.

Those origins remain visible in this repository. They are not being erased. They mark the path by which a dental AI infrastructure question became a broader scientific question about Human-AI Clinical Cognition.
