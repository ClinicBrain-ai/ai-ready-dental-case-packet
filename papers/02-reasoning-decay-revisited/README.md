# Reasoning Decay Revisited

Positioning: testable research hypothesis paper for the Clinical Cognition Transformation Lab (CCTL).

## Abstract

The earlier concept of Reasoning Decay asked whether clinical reasoning might weaken when clinicians increasingly rely on AI systems for interpretation, summary, recommendation, or decision support. That concern remains important, but it is incomplete if treated only as a story of loss. Long-term AI use may reduce some forms of recall, compress intermediate reasoning, or shift responsibility for specific cognitive tasks. It may also amplify metacognitive supervision, create new forms of expertise, or redistribute cognition across clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, and institutional workflows.

This paper revisits Reasoning Decay within the broader CCTL framework of Clinical Cognition Transformation. The central question is not whether AI necessarily harms clinician expertise. The central question is how clinician cognition, expertise development, and reasoning structure change as AI becomes embedded in everyday clinical practice. The paper proposes mechanisms and study designs for distinguishing skill atrophy from cognitive delegation, cognitive redistribution, expertise transformation, and metacognitive amplification.

## Background

Clinical expertise is not a fixed inventory of facts and procedures. It includes pattern recognition, causal reasoning, uncertainty management, memory, attention, judgment, explanation, documentation, coordination, and the ability to notice when a case does not fit an expected frame. These capacities develop through repeated engagement with clinical problems, feedback, mentorship, institutional norms, and patient outcomes.

AI systems are now entering this developmental environment. They may summarize records, draft notes, suggest differential diagnoses, rank risks, propose treatment options, and translate clinical explanations. Over time, these systems may change not only clinical tasks but also the cognitive routines through which clinicians learn, remember, infer, and supervise.

Reasoning Decay was an early attempt to name one possible consequence: the erosion of reasoning pathways when downstream artifacts preserve conclusions without preserving how those conclusions were produced. CCTL retains that concern but broadens the analytic frame. What looks like decay may sometimes be loss. It may also be transformation.

## The Original Reasoning Decay Hypothesis

The original Reasoning Decay hypothesis can be stated simply: when clinical systems repeatedly preserve answers, labels, plans, or recommendations while omitting the reasoning that produced them, clinicians and downstream actors may lose access to the cognitive pathway needed for understanding, critique, and accountability.

In AI-mediated environments, this concern becomes sharper. A clinician may receive an AI-generated summary without seeing the selection logic. A trainee may accept a differential diagnosis without practicing the intermediate steps of hypothesis generation. A treatment plan may inherit a recommendation whose uncertainty has been compressed. A consultation note may carry forward a conclusion while the causal chain has disappeared.

The hypothesis is plausible, but it should not be treated as settled. It risks becoming too narrow if it assumes that any reduction in visible reasoning is necessarily harmful or that all AI-mediated change is decay. Clinical cognition has always involved delegation, compression, and artifact-mediated work. The empirical challenge is to determine which transformations reduce expertise, which preserve it, and which create new forms of expertise.

## From Reasoning Decay to Cognitive Transformation

CCTL reframes Reasoning Decay as one possible form of Clinical Cognition Transformation. The broader question is how cognition changes when clinical work becomes distributed across humans, AI systems, documents, workflows, institutions, patients, and caregivers.

This reframing matters because AI may change cognition in multiple directions at once. A clinician may recall fewer rare drug interactions while becoming better at supervising generated recommendations. A trainee may perform fewer unguided differential diagnoses while becoming more skilled at recognizing when AI output is overconfident. A specialist may write shorter notes because AI drafts structured summaries, yet spend more effort evaluating whether the summary preserved uncertainty.

These examples do not prove benefit or harm. They show why the phenomenon should be studied as transformation rather than presumed decay. The task is to distinguish cognitive loss from cognitive redistribution, and to identify the conditions under which each occurs.

## Possible Mechanisms

Several mechanisms may explain how long-term AI use changes clinician cognition.

First, repeated exposure to AI-generated interpretations may reduce practice in first-pass reasoning. Clinicians may generate fewer independent hypotheses before seeing suggested diagnoses or summaries.

Second, AI-mediated summaries may change attention. Information surfaced by a system may become more salient, while omitted or downranked information may be less likely to shape clinical judgment.

Third, documentation may shift from active reasoning expression to verification, editing, or correction of generated text. This may alter how clinicians externalize and refine their reasoning.

Fourth, uncertainty may be compressed when probabilistic, tentative, or contested reasoning is converted into concise recommendations or operational plans.

Fifth, oversight may become a distinct cognitive skill. Clinicians may learn to inspect AI output for missing assumptions, inappropriate confidence, unsupported causal claims, or mismatch with patient context.

These mechanisms are testable. They should be studied across roles, specialties, levels of expertise, and types of AI integration.

## Cognitive Delegation

Cognitive delegation occurs when a cognitive task previously performed by a clinician is assigned partly or fully to another actor, system, or artifact. In AI-mediated care, delegation may include chart summarization, image pre-interpretation, risk stratification, patient message drafting, or generation of preliminary treatment options.

Delegation is not automatically decay. Medicine has always depended on distributed labor, instruments, records, protocols, and teams. The relevant questions are more specific: Which cognitive tasks are delegated? Which tasks remain human? What supervision is required? What knowledge is needed to detect errors? What happens when delegated tasks are no longer practiced regularly?

Reasoning Decay becomes empirically interesting when delegation reduces the clinician's ability to reconstruct, critique, or override the delegated process. Cognitive delegation becomes beneficial when it preserves or improves the system's ability to notice, explain, and act responsibly.

## Expertise Compression

Expertise compression occurs when complex reasoning is condensed into more compact forms: a ranked differential, a short summary, a treatment recommendation, a templated note, or a decision pathway. AI systems may accelerate this compression by generating plausible outputs from large bodies of data.

Compression can support care by reducing cognitive burden and making action possible under time constraints. It can also erase intermediate reasoning, uncertainty, and minority possibilities. The key research question is not whether compression occurs, but what is lost and what is preserved when it occurs.

In education, expertise compression may be especially important. Trainees need exposure to intermediate reasoning steps to develop durable clinical judgment. Experts may rely on compressed representations while retaining enough background knowledge to unpack them. The same AI tool may therefore affect trainees and experts differently.

## Uncertainty Offloading

Uncertainty offloading occurs when the burden of representing, managing, or communicating uncertainty shifts from the clinician to an AI system, a documentation artifact, or a workflow. For example, a model may rank options, assign risk levels, or generate a confident narrative where the underlying evidence remains ambiguous.

Offloading uncertainty may help clinicians manage complex information. It may also hide the fact that uncertainty exists. When uncertainty is converted into a recommendation, downstream actors may inherit action without the caution that originally accompanied it.

Long-term AI use may therefore alter clinicians' relationship to uncertainty. Some clinicians may become less practiced at generating uncertainty estimates independently. Others may become more skilled at challenging AI confidence, identifying missing caveats, and asking for alternative interpretations. Both outcomes are plausible. Both should be studied.

## Skill Atrophy vs Skill Transformation

Skill atrophy refers to a genuine decline in a clinician's ability to perform a cognitive task. Skill transformation refers to a change in the structure or target of expertise. The distinction matters.

If clinicians become less able to generate independent differentials after prolonged reliance on AI suggestions, that may represent atrophy. If clinicians generate fewer independent differentials but become better at evaluating AI-generated differentials, detecting unsafe omissions, and integrating patient-specific constraints, that may represent transformation. If the system as a whole performs better while individual unaided performance declines, the ethical, educational, and safety implications become more complex.

This paper does not assume that AI necessarily harms expertise. It asks how expertise changes, which changes are desirable, which are risky, and which are hidden by current evaluation methods.

## Proposed Study Designs

Several empirical approaches could test the Reasoning Decay Revisited framework.

One design is a longitudinal clinician study comparing reasoning tasks before and after repeated AI use. Participants could complete unaided reasoning tasks, AI-assisted tasks, and oversight tasks at multiple time points. Outcomes would include diagnostic accuracy, reasoning completeness, uncertainty representation, error detection, and calibration.

A second design is a trainee-versus-expert comparison. Trainees and experienced clinicians may show different patterns of delegation, compression, and oversight. This design could test whether AI affects expertise development differently from expert performance.

A third design is documentation analysis. Researchers could compare clinician-authored notes, AI-drafted notes, edited AI notes, and downstream consultation notes to identify changes in reasoning visibility, uncertainty preservation, and treatment intent.

A fourth design is artifact-pair reconstruction. Researchers could examine whether reasoning can be reconstructed from downstream documents after AI mediation, and whether reconstruction quality changes over time.

A fifth design is simulated workflow experimentation. Synthetic cases could vary the timing, confidence, and content of AI recommendations to test how clinicians revise, defer, or override their own reasoning.

Together, these study designs would allow CCTL to distinguish simple performance effects from deeper changes in clinical cognition.

## Discussion

Reasoning Decay remains a useful warning concept, but it should not become a predetermined conclusion. The history of clinical practice is already a history of cognitive distribution: records, imaging, laboratory systems, protocols, teams, and specialist consultations have long shaped how clinicians think. AI intensifies this distribution and introduces new forms of cognitive participation, but it does not create distribution from nothing.

The most important risk may not be that clinicians simply stop thinking. The more subtle risk is that thinking changes in ways that are difficult to observe: intermediate reasoning disappears, uncertainty becomes less visible, documentation becomes more fluent but less informative, and accountability becomes harder to reconstruct. At the same time, new expertise may emerge around supervision, critique, calibration, and human-AI coordination.

The research challenge is to describe these changes without alarmism or technological utopianism. CCTL's contribution is to treat clinician cognition as dynamic within distributed human-AI systems.

## Conclusion

Reasoning Decay Revisited asks whether long-term AI use changes clinician cognition, expertise development, and reasoning structure. The answer is unlikely to be a simple yes or no.

AI may reduce some forms of recall while amplifying metacognitive supervision. It may compress intermediate reasoning steps while creating new oversight demands. It may shift clinicians from first-order reasoning toward second-order evaluation. Some observed decay may represent genuine skill loss, while other patterns may represent cognitive delegation, redistribution, or transformation.

The goal is not to assume harm. The goal is to study how expertise changes as clinical cognition becomes distributed. That question is central to CCTL's broader research program on Human-AI Clinical Cognition.
