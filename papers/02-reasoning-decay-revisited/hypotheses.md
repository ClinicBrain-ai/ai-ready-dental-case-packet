# Reasoning Decay Revisited: Hypotheses

## Primary Hypotheses

1. AI may reduce some forms of recall while amplifying metacognitive supervision.

   Clinicians who repeatedly use AI systems may rely less on unaided recall for some facts, patterns, or pathways, while becoming more practiced at evaluating the quality, confidence, and appropriateness of AI output.

2. AI may compress intermediate reasoning steps.

   AI-generated summaries, differentials, recommendations, and treatment plans may preserve conclusions while reducing visibility into the intermediate reasoning that produced them.

3. AI may shift clinicians from first-order reasoning toward second-order oversight.

   Clinicians may spend less effort generating every possible interpretation independently and more effort supervising, critiquing, calibrating, and contextualizing AI-generated interpretations.

4. Some observed decay may actually represent redistribution of cognition.

   Apparent declines in unaided reasoning performance may reflect movement of cognitive labor across clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, and workflows rather than simple loss of expertise.

## Secondary Hypotheses

1. Different specialties may transform differently.

   Image-heavy, procedure-heavy, longitudinal, emergency, and primary care settings may show different patterns of delegation, compression, uncertainty offloading, and oversight.

2. Experts and trainees may be affected differently.

   Experts may use AI to accelerate already-developed reasoning pathways, while trainees may experience altered exposure to the intermediate reasoning steps needed for expertise development.

3. Documentation behavior may change before diagnostic behavior changes.

   Clinicians may first shift how they write, edit, summarize, and preserve reasoning before measurable changes appear in diagnostic accuracy or treatment selection.

4. New forms of expertise may emerge.

   AI-embedded practice may create expertise in supervision, prompt framing, uncertainty interrogation, output calibration, cognitive provenance review, and human-AI workflow coordination.

## Measurement Implications

- Studies should measure unaided reasoning, AI-assisted reasoning, and AI oversight separately.
- Studies should distinguish diagnostic accuracy from reasoning visibility, uncertainty preservation, and accountability.
- Studies should compare short-term performance effects with longitudinal expertise change.
- Studies should examine documentation and artifact transitions, not only final clinical decisions.
- Studies should treat skill atrophy, cognitive delegation, cognitive redistribution, and expertise transformation as competing explanations to be tested.
